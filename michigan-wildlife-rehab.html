The Geography of Wildlife Care in Michigan

Using GIS to explore how access to wildlife rehabilitation varies across Michigan and why location matters for both wildlife and the people working to protect it.

Project Overview

This StoryMap explores the distribution of licensed wildlife rehabilitation centers across Michigan and examines how geography influences access to wildlife care.

Using GIS, it reveals spatial patterns that support a deeper understanding of Michigan's wildlife rehabilitation network and opportunities for future conservation planning.

Every Point Represents a Place of Hope

Maps often reduce places to points, lines, and polygons, but behind every point on this map is a person dedicated to helping wildlife.

Licensed wildlife rehabilitators answer emergency phone calls, provide medical care, raise orphaned animals, educate their communities, and devote countless hours to giving injured wildlife the opportunity to return to the wild.

Much of this work is volunteer-driven, emotionally demanding, and often unseen by the public.

This StoryMap is about more than the locations of wildlife rehabilitation centers.

It is about recognizing the network of people whose knowledge, compassion, and dedication make wildlife rehabilitation possible throughout Michigan.

By viewing these facilities through the lens of GIS, we gain a broader understanding of where wildlife care is readily available, where geographic gaps remain, and how spatial analysis can help support future conservation efforts.

Every point on this map represents a place where someone has chosen to invest their time, expertise, and compassion in giving Michigan's wildlife another chance.

Conservation is measured not only by the landscapes we protect, but also by the people who choose to care for the wildlife that depends on them.

Key Insights

  • Rehabilitation centers are most heavily concentrated in southern Michigan.
  • Geographic access to wildlife care decreases in many northern and rural areas.
  • Population density and transportation networks appear closely related to rehabilitation center locations.
  • GIS provides a valuable framework for identifying patterns in wildlife care accessibility and informing future conservation efforts.
  • Every point represents a dedicated rehabilitator providing care, education, and a second chance for Michigan's Wildlife.
