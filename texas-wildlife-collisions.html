Wildlife Collisions and the Geography of Care in Texas

Exploring where wildlife-vehicle collisions occur and how access to rehabilitation shapes outcomes across a vast and ecologically diverse landscape.

Project Overview

This project examines spatial patterns of wildlife-vehicle collisions across Texas and evaluates how accessible wildlife rehabilitation resources are relative to those events.

The goal was not only to map where collisions occur, but to understand how infrastructure, habitat connectivity, and human activity shape those patterns.

Reflection

A Map Is Never the End of the Story

When this project began, the goal was to explore where wildlife-vehicle collisions occur across Texas and whether injured wildlife has meaningful access to rehabilitation.

The maps answered many of those questions.

They also revealed new ones.

They showed that wildlife collisions cannot be explained by population density alone. They highlighted the influence of transportation networks, the importance of habitat connectivity, and the uneven accessibility of rehabilitation resources across a landscape as vast and ecologically diverse as Texas.

Perhaps more importantly, they demonstrated something beyond the numbers.

Every point displayed throughout this StoryMap represents an encounter between people and wildlife.

Someone noticed an injured hawk beside a highway.

A family stopped to report a turtle along the shoulder of a rural road.

A rehabilitator prepared to receive an animal that otherwise might never have reached care.

These moments often last only minutes.

Collectively, they become data.

And from that data emerge patterns that help us better understand the landscapes we share with wildlife.

This is the role of GIS in conservation.

Not simply to create maps.

But to reveal relationships that inform better decisions, inspire new questions, and strengthen our ability to care for the natural world.

At EcoGIS Studio, every project begins with curiosity.

Every map is an opportunity to better understand the connections between wildlife, landscapes, and people.

And every story is part of a much larger one—one that continues to unfold each time someone steps outside, makes an observation, and chooses to look a little more closely at the world around them.

Because conservation does not begin with a map. It begins with paying attention.

Key Insights

  • Wildlife-vehicle collisions are not explained by population density alone.
  • Transportation corridors strongly shape collision hotspots.
  • Access to rehabilitation varies significantly across regions.
  • Every data point represents a real encounter between people and wildlife.
